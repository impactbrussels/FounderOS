# Naming & Brand Reference

Deeper frameworks behind the `brand-and-naming` skill. Theme-agnostic; the founder supplies the
domain. **Availability cannot be confirmed by an AI** — this reference tells you what to check; you
verify with real tools and, for trademarks, a lawyer.

## What a name must do (the bar)

A good startup name is, in priority order:
1. **Sayable** — survives being spoken aloud, over a phone, in a noisy room.
2. **Spellable** — typed correctly after hearing it once. ("Is that with a 'k'?" loses customers.)
3. **Memorable & distinct** — stands apart from competitors; not generic.
4. **Available** — domain, trademark, and the handles you need (must be verified).
5. **Right-fit** — matches positioning for `[ICP]` and doesn't box the company in.

A name does not need to be brilliant or self-explanatory. Google and Spotify meant nothing until
the product gave them meaning. Clear and ownable beats clever and contested.

## Naming-style menu (generate across all of these)

| Style | What it is | Example pattern | Trade-off |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Descriptive** | Says what it does | "QuickBooks", "Pizza Hut" | Clear; hard to trademark; boxes you in as you expand |
| **Suggestive** | Evokes the benefit/feeling | "Salesforce", "Slack" | Ownable + meaningful; needs a little marketing |
| **Abstract / coined** | Made-up word | "Kodak", "Spotify" | Fully ownable, easy domain/TM; means nothing until you build it |
| **Founder / namesake** | A person's name | "Dell", "Warby Parker (literary)" | Personal, trustworthy; harder to sell/exit; ties brand to a person |
| **Compound** | Two real words joined | "Facebook", "Snapchat" | Memorable, often available; can feel forced |
| **Real-word / borrowed** | An existing word, repurposed | "Apple", "Amazon" | Friendly, easy to say; trademark + domain usually contested |

Generate candidates in *every* style so you compare real alternatives, not one idea's variations.

## Screening checklist (run every candidate)

For each shortlisted name, check and record:

- [ ] **Sayable** — say it out loud 3×. Awkward? Cut it.
- [ ] **Spellable** — would someone type it right after only hearing it? No silent letters/ambiguous spellings.
- [ ] **Memorable** — recall it an hour later without looking.
- [ ] **Distinct** — not a near-clone of a competitor; won't be confused.
- [ ] **Connotation (multilingual)** — no unfortunate or offensive meaning in the **languages of your
      target markets**. Check this seriously; it has sunk real product launches.
- [ ] **Length / shape** — short enough to remember and type; works as a logo and a handle.
- [ ] **Availability — VERIFY, never assume:**
  - **Domain** — check a registrar directly. Decide if you need the exact .com or can live with an alt.
  - **Trademark** — search the relevant database(s) for **your** jurisdiction(s) and class(es). This is
    where a lawyer is worth it; conflicts are expensive to discover late.
  - **Social handles** — check the platforms you'll actually use.
  - **Search collision** — does it already return strong, confusing results?

> Record availability as **"UNVERIFIED — check [registrar / TM database / handles]"** until you have
> actually confirmed it. Never write "available" on the founder's behalf.

## Brand-voice template

> **[STARTUP_NAME] sounds like** [a person/archetype — e.g. "a calm expert friend"]. With **[ICP]**
> it speaks in a **[TONE]** voice: [2-3 adjectives]. It makes the reader feel **[feeling]**. It never
> sounds **[the wrong note to avoid]**.

Then make it operational:

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| (e.g.) Say it plainly, short sentences | Use hype words ("revolutionary", "game-changing") |
| Speak to "you", lead with the benefit | Talk about ourselves first |
| Use concrete specifics | Hide behind jargon and buzzwords |

3 do + 3 don't rules are enough to keep a landing page, emails, and posts sounding like one company.

## Anti-patterns

- **Unspellable / unsayable** — clever spellings ("Lyft", "Flickr") work for a few; for most they leak customers.
- **Hard to find** — a name that collides in search or whose domain/handles are all taken.
- **Boxes you in** — over-descriptive names ("CheapBooksOnline") that trap the company in one niche.
- **Trademark blind spot** — falling in love before checking; the most expensive naming mistake.
- **Connotation blind spot** — ignoring meanings in your other market languages.
- **Bikeshedding** — burning weeks debating names while the product waits. Set the bar, screen, decide, move.
- **AI-confirmed availability** — trusting any tool (including this one) that *claims* a name is free. Verify.

## Further reading (verify against primary sources)

*Hello, My Name Is Awesome* (Alexandra Watkins) · *Don't Call It That* (Eli Altman) · the legal
basics of trademark classes for your jurisdiction (consult a trademark attorney). Treat naming books
as method; treat all availability and legal questions as things to **verify with real tools and a
lawyer**, never assume.
