# Brand & Naming

Naming paralyses first-time founders for weeks — and the wrong name is expensive to undo once a
domain, logo, and reputation are attached to it. A name doesn't have to be brilliant; it has to be
**clear, sayable, findable, and free to use**. This skill clarifies what the name must do, generates
a shortlist across naming styles, screens it hard (including the availability checks the founder
must run themselves), and then locks in a simple brand voice so everything written under the name
sounds like the same company.

> **Availability is not something an AI can confirm.** Domain, trademark, and handle availability
> change constantly and vary by jurisdiction. This skill tells you *what* to check and *how* — you
> (and, for trademarks, a lawyer) must verify it with real tools before committing.

## The method

Built on the FounderOS scaffold (High tier). Full naming-style menu, screening checklist and
voice template in [references/naming.md](references/naming.md).

### Step 1 — Clarify what the name must do

Before generating anything, fix the constraints. A good name is **memorable, sayable, spellable,
and available**, and it fits `[STARTUP_NAME]`'s positioning for `[ICP]`. Decide:
- How descriptive vs. distinctive should it be? (Descriptive is clear but hard to trademark and
  boxes you in; distinctive is ownable but needs marketing to give it meaning.)
- Any must-haves or hard nos? (Geography, language markets it must work in, length, ".com" or not.)
This step prevents falling in love with a name that can't do the job.

### Step 2 — Generate a shortlist across naming styles

Don't brainstorm in one style. Use the naming-style menu in the reference — descriptive, suggestive,
abstract/coined, founder, compound, real-word — and generate candidates in *each*, so you compare
genuinely different options instead of variations on one idea. Aim for a shortlist of 8-12 to screen,
not one favourite to defend.

### Step 3 — Screen for clarity, connotation, and availability

Run each candidate through the screening checklist in the reference:
- **Sayable & spellable** — can someone say it out loud and type it correctly after hearing it once?
- **Memorable & distinct** — does it stand out, or blur into competitors?
- **Connotation** — any unfortunate meanings, especially across the **languages of your markets**?
- **Available** — and here is the hard rule: **you must actually check.** The AI will tell you what
  to verify (domain registrar, trademark database for your jurisdictions, the social handles you'll
  use) and flag risks, but it **cannot and must not claim a name is available.** Treat every name as
  unavailable until you've confirmed it with real tools, and the trademark with a lawyer.

### Step 4 — Define a one-paragraph brand voice

A name is half the brand; how it speaks is the other half. Write a **one-paragraph voice
definition** — who `[STARTUP_NAME]` sounds like, the `[TONE]` it uses with `[ICP]`, and the feeling
it should leave. Then make it operational with **3 do / 3 don't tone rules** (e.g. "Do: say it
plainly. Don't: use hype words"). Use the voice template in the reference. This is what keeps your
landing page, emails, and posts sounding like one company.

## Output

- **Naming brief:** what the name must do (constraints, must-haves, hard nos, descriptive vs. distinctive).
- **Shortlist:** 8-12 candidates, labelled by naming style.
- **Screening table:** each candidate scored on sayable / spellable / memorable / connotation, with a
  clear **"availability: UNVERIFIED — check X, Y, Z"** flag and the exact tools to use. No availability claims.
- **Brand voice:** one paragraph + 3 do / 3 don't tone rules.
- **Next action:** the availability/trademark checks the founder must run before committing.

## Constraints

- **Never claim a name (or domain/handle/trademark) is available.** State what to check and where; the
  founder verifies with real tools, and a lawyer for trademarks.
- **Clear beats clever.** If people can't say it, spell it, or find it, it's a bad name however cool it sounds.
- **Check connotations across the languages of your actual markets** — not just English.
- **Don't box yourself in** — an over-descriptive name limits where the company can go later.
- Choosing a name is a **higher-care, hard-to-reverse decision** (domain, legal, reputation attach to it) —
  slow down for the availability and trademark step.

## Copy-paste version

```
Act as a sharp brand and naming coach for a first-time founder. Never tell me a name, domain, handle,
or trademark is "available" — you can't know that. Tell me exactly what to check and where, and treat
every name as unverified until I confirm it (and a lawyer confirms the trademark).
My startup: [ONE_LINER]. Who it's for: [ICP]. Voice/tone I want: [TONE].
Help me:
1. Define what the name must do (memorable, sayable, spellable, available) and decide how descriptive vs.
   distinctive it should be — note any must-haves or hard nos.
2. Generate a shortlist of 8-12 candidates ACROSS naming styles (descriptive, suggestive, abstract/coined,
   founder, compound, real-word), labelled by style.
3. Screen each for sayable/spellable, memorable, and connotation (across the languages of my markets), and
   for each flag exactly what availability I must verify (domain, trademark for my jurisdiction, social
   handles) — without claiming anything is free.
4. Write a one-paragraph brand voice + 3 do / 3 don't tone rules so everything I write sounds like one company.
Make me slow down before committing, and remind me a name is hard to undo.
```
