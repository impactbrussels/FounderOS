# Raise Process (reference)

The frameworks behind the `investor-pipeline` skill. Theme-agnostic; the founder supplies the
domain and the numbers. The core idea: **a raise is a sales pipeline.** Treat investors as
qualified leads, move them through stages, and manufacture urgency by running them in parallel.

> IMPORTANT: Fundraising **instruments and terms** - SAFEs, convertible notes, priced rounds,
> valuation caps, discounts, pro-rata, option pools, liquidation preferences, KYC/AML and what
> "standard" even means - **vary significantly by country and by stage.** Everything here is
> *process*, not legal or financial advice. Confirm every instrument and term with a qualified
> lawyer/advisor in your jurisdiction before signing anything.

## Step 0 - The raise decision

Answer before building any list:

| Question | If the answer is weak… |
|----------|------------------------|
| Should I raise at all (vs. revenue / grant / smaller scope)? | …don't default to raising. |
| Why now - what does the money buy? | …you'll get a vague ask and soft nos. |
| How much, tied to which milestone? | …"some money to grow" loses investors. |
| Do I have the evidence this stage expects? | …raising too early burns credibility. |

Size the raise to **reach the next inflection + runway buffer**, not to a round-number vibe.

## The investor-pipeline tracker

Single source of truth. One row per investor. Move them left → right.

| Investor / Firm | Stage fit | Sector fit | Check size fit | Warm path | Pipeline stage | Last touch | Next action | Notes |
|-----------------|-----------|-----------|----------------|-----------|----------------|------------|-------------|-------|
| [Firm A] | ✓ leads pre-seed | ✓ | $X fits | via [founder] | first meeting | 12 Jun | send metrics | likes the team |
| [Angel B] | ✓ | ✓ | small | direct | intro | 10 Jun | book call | fast mover |
| [Fund C] | ✗ later stage | ✓ | too big | - | research | - | deprioritise | wrong stage |

**Pipeline stages (the funnel):**

`research → intro → first meeting → partner meeting → due diligence (DD) → term sheet`

- **research** - qualified and on the list, not yet contacted.
- **intro** - warm intro requested/sent, or cold outreach out.
- **first meeting** - initial call done; they're interested enough to continue.
- **partner meeting** - championed inside the firm to the full partnership (the real gate).
- **DD** - diligence: metrics, references, data room.
- **term sheet** - offer on the table. (Terms = lawyer territory; see note above.)

Expect heavy drop-off at each stage - that's why the list must be deep enough to run a real
process, not three names.

## Qualifying a target (do this before adding to the list)

- **Stage** - do they *lead* or participate at your stage? A growth fund won't do your pre-seed.
- **Sector** - have they invested in your space (or explicitly want to)?
- **Check size** - does their typical cheque match your round and ownership target?
- **Portfolio fit** - strong adjacency = good; direct competitor in portfolio = likely conflict.
- **Decision speed / reputation** - founder-friendly? Fast? Ask their portfolio founders.

Go for **fit, not volume.** Twenty well-matched investors beat two hundred sprayed cold.

## Outreach templates

### Warm-intro request (to your connector - make it forwardable)

> Subject: Quick intro to [Investor]?
>
> Hi [Connector] - we're raising a [STAGE] round for [STARTUP_NAME] ([ONE_LINER]). I think
> [Investor] could be a great fit because [specific reason - stage/sector/thesis].
> Would you be open to a forwardable intro? I've written a blurb below so it's one click for you.
>
> **Forwardable blurb:** [STARTUP_NAME] helps [ICP] [outcome]. We've [strongest traction in one
> line]. Raising [amount] to [GOAL]. Deck attached. Would love 20 minutes with [Investor].

Make it genuinely *forwardable* - your connector should be able to send it without rewriting.

### Cold outreach (fallback - short, specific, no fluff)

> Subject: [STARTUP_NAME] - [ONE_LINER] (raising [STAGE])
>
> Hi [Investor] - I'm reaching out because you [specific, real reason: led X, wrote about Y,
> back Z-type companies]. [STARTUP_NAME] helps [ICP] [outcome]. The signal so far: [one strong
> metric/traction line]. We're raising [amount] to [GOAL]. Could I send a deck or grab 20 minutes?

Cold works only when the "why you specifically" line is real. Generic blasts get ignored.

## Process tactics - running a tight timeline

- **Batch the window.** Cluster first meetings into ~2-3 weeks so investors move in parallel.
  Parallel pipelines create competitive tension; serial meetings create silence.
- **Warm up before you hit priority targets.** Take 2-4 lower-priority meetings first to sharpen
  the pitch; meet your top choices when you're at your best.
- **Set a soft close date** and (honestly) let investors know you're running a process. Urgency
  closes rounds; open-ended timelines stall them.
- **Fast follow-up.** Send anything promised (metrics, references) within 24h. Speed signals a
  founder who executes.
- **Track everything** in the tracker; never let an interested investor go cold from neglect.
- **Get to the partner meeting.** A single associate's enthusiasm isn't a yes - the partnership
  is the real gate. Ask your champion what they need to win the room.

## Data-room checklist (have it ready before DD, not during)

- One-pager + current pitch deck.
- Cap table (current + post-round, simplified).
- Financial model + historical financials / burn + runway.
- Key metrics dashboard (growth, retention/cohorts, unit economics).
- Customer evidence (LOIs, pilots, references, logos).
- Team bios + org/hiring plan.
- Product overview / demo link / roadmap.
- Corporate basics (incorporation, key contracts, IP) - *scope and format vary by jurisdiction;
  ask your lawyer what's expected locally.*
- Use of funds tied to the milestone the raise buys.

A tidy data room shortens DD and signals operational maturity. A scramble mid-DD kills momentum.

## Monthly investor-update email template

Send to current *and* prospective investors. Consistency turns "not now" into "yes" over time.

> **Subject:** [STARTUP_NAME] update - [Month Year]
>
> **TL;DR:** [one line - the headline of the month].
>
> **Metrics:** [METRIC] [value] ([+/- vs last month]); [2-3 more key numbers].
>
> **Wins:** [shipped X / closed Y / hired Z].
>
> **Lowlights / challenges:** [be honest - this builds trust more than only-good-news].
>
> **Asks:** [specific help - intros to [target], a hire, a customer]. Make it easy to act on.
>
> **Runway:** [months left]. **Raise status (if relevant):** [open / closing soon].

Honest lowlights and a specific ask outperform glossy "everything's great" updates every time.

## Anti-patterns (the ways raises go wrong)

- **Raising too early** - pitching before you have the evidence the stage expects burns
  credibility you can't easily win back. Fix the evidence first.
- **Serial one-off meetings** - dribbling meetings over months kills urgency and momentum;
  parallel + batched creates competition.
- **Vague ask** - "raising some money to grow" reads as unprepared. State amount + milestone +
  timeline.
- **Volume over fit** - spraying 200 mismatched investors wastes the window; 20 well-matched
  beats it.
- **Burning top targets first** - pitching your dream investor before the pitch is sharp.
- **No tracker** - interested investors go cold from neglect; you lose the thread.
- **Confusing an associate's interest with a yes** - the partnership is the gate.
- **Going dark between rounds** - no updates means prospective investors forget you exist.
- **Signing terms you don't understand** - instruments/terms vary by jurisdiction; a lawyer
  reviews before you sign, every time.
