# The pivot playbook

The frameworks behind `pivot-decision`. The decision is hard because the forces pulling you are
emotional (sunk cost, hope, identity) and the evidence is quiet. The job is to make the quiet
evidence louder than the feelings, then decide on purpose.

## Did versus said: the only sort that matters

Founders drown in ambiguous signal. Cut it in two columns.

- **Did**: they paid, pre-paid, returned, referred, used it again unprompted, introduced you to the
  budget holder. Behaviour that cost them something.
- **Said**: "love it", "I'd definitely buy", "great idea", "keep me posted". Politeness, free.

Only the **did** column is evidence. A pile of **said** with an empty **did** column is the single
most common way a dead idea looks alive.

## Score against the pre-set bar

In `validate-idea` and `north-star-metrics` you committed to a pass/fail number before you ran the
test. The pivot call holds the result against **that** bar. The failure mode is the moving goalpost:
the bar was "20 pre-orders", you got 3, and now the story is "but the 3 are so engaged." Three
engaged users can be real signal for a zoom-in, but only if you call it that on purpose, not if you
quietly redefine success to whatever you happened to get.

## The persevere / pivot / kill logic

- **Persevere** when the leading indicators are moving toward the bar, even if slowly, and you have
  runway to reach it. Keep the course; tighten the loop.
- **Pivot** when one part is clearly validated and one part is clearly not. You keep the validated
  part and change the failing one. Pivots are *surgical*, not total.
- **Kill** when the core problem-or-demand bet has been tested cheaply and honestly and failed, or
  when runway can no longer fund another real test. Killing well frees the founder and the capital
  for a better bet; a slow death wastes both.

## The pivot taxonomy (Eric Ries, The Lean Startup)

Name the pivot or you have not made a decision. The common types:

| Pivot | Keep | Change |
|-------|------|--------|
| Zoom-in | One feature that users love | Drop the rest; the feature becomes the product |
| Zoom-out | The need | Your product becomes one feature of a bigger whole |
| Customer-segment | The problem and solution | Aim them at a different buyer who feels it more |
| Customer-need | The customer | Solve a different, adjacent problem they have |
| Platform | The solution | App to platform, or platform to app |
| Channel | The product | A different route to the same buyer |
| Business-architecture | The tech | High-margin/low-volume to low-margin/high-volume, or back |

A change that does not map to one of these, and keeps almost nothing validated, is a **restart**:
a new idea. The honest move is to admit that and re-run `validate-idea`, not to spend the credibility
of "we're pivoting" on starting from zero.

## The kill checklist (when the call is stop)

- Have you tested the **cheapest** version of the core bet, or just an expensive one badly?
- Did it fail on the **bar you pre-set**, or on a vibe?
- Is the constraint **runway** (out of swings) or **evidence** (the bet is wrong)? Name which.
- Have you told a second human, ideally one who is not emotionally invested?
- What did you learn that makes the next bet better? Log it with `capture-learning`.

Killing is not failing. Building the wrong thing for two more years is.

## The tripwire

Every decision gets a **review date** and a single metric that, if it crosses a line, forces a
re-look. "Revisit in 6 weeks; if weekly active is still under X, we pivot the segment." Without a
tripwire, persevere quietly becomes drift, and the decision you thought you made decays into
not-deciding.

## A note on honesty

This skill exists to defeat motivated reasoning, including the AI's. If you ask a model to justify
continuing, it will; if you ask it to justify quitting, it will. Make it argue **both** sides from
the same evidence, then decide. The founder owns the call; the framework only makes it defensible.
