# Sensory Testing

People can *say* they'd buy your product and still spit out the first bite. For anything tasted,
smelled, felt, or applied to the body, opinion surveys and interviews aren't enough - you need
**sensory evidence**, gathered under conditions that stop the result from lying to you. This is
the discipline food, beverage, and personal-care companies use, scaled down so a first-time
founder can run it. Proof before hype: a blind, controlled test beats a founder's own palate
every time.

## The method

Built on the Founder OS scaffold (High tier - this drives formulation and launch decisions).
Full protocols, scales, and sample sizes in [references/sensory-methods.md](references/sensory-methods.md).

### Step 1 - Decide what question you're actually asking

Match the test to the decision. The three families (see references for protocols):

| Your question | Test family | Example method |
|---------------|-------------|----------------|
| "Are these two versions *different* at all?" | **Discrimination** | Triangle test, duo-trio, paired-comparison |
| "*How* do they differ - describe the profile" | **Descriptive** | Trained-panel profiling / QDA, CATA |
| "Do target consumers *like* it / which do they prefer?" | **Affective (acceptance)** | 9-point hedonic scale, paired preference, JAR |

Discrimination and descriptive use small *trained/screened* panels; acceptance needs *untrained
target consumers* (your [ICP]) - never mix the two jobs.

### Step 2 - Control the bias, or don't bother

A sloppy taste test produces confident nonsense. Non-negotiables (details in references):
- **Blind & coded** - samples labelled with random 3-digit codes, never "A/B" or brand names.
- **Randomised & balanced** serving order (counterbalance to cancel first-sample bias).
- **Consistent conditions** - same temperature, portion, vessel, lighting; neutral palate
  cleanser (water/cracker) between samples; separated tasting so people don't influence each other.
- **Blind to your hope** - whoever runs it shouldn't telegraph the "right" answer.

### Step 3 - Size it and set the rule *before* you taste

Pick the sample size and the pass/fail rule up front (references give the tables):
- *Discrimination:* enough assessors to reach significance (e.g. a triangle test needs a set
  number of correct identifications for n tasters - use the reference table, don't eyeball).
- *Acceptance:* enough target consumers for a stable liking score (a screening read often starts
  around 50-100 target consumers; treat small panels as directional, not proof).

### Step 4 - Run it, then read it honestly

Collect scores, compare against the pre-set rule, and report the *result*, not the hope. "No
significant difference" is a real, useful answer (e.g. your cheaper formula is sensorily
equivalent). A liking score only means something against a **benchmark** - your current product
or the market leader, tested blind in the same session.

## Output

- **Test design:** the question, chosen method, sample type (trained vs target consumer), sample
  size, controls, and the pre-set decision rule.
- **Run sheet:** coded samples, serving order, score sheet/scale, conditions.
- **Result read:** what the data says, significance/benchmark, and the formulation or go/no-go
  decision it supports - with assumptions flagged.

## Constraints

- **Acceptance ≠ discrimination.** Don't ask untrained consumers to describe; don't ask a trained
  panel whether they "like" it. Different jobs, different people.
- **Always blind, always benchmarked.** An unblinded or un-benchmarked score is marketing, not data.
- **Small panels are directional.** Don't over-claim from 8 tasters; say so.
- **Safety first.** For anything ingested or applied to skin, confirm it's safe to test and meet
  the relevant food-safety / cosmetic-safety and consent requirements *before* anyone tries it -
  verify with a qualified expert; this skill is method guidance, not safety or regulatory advice.
- Stay theme-agnostic across consumable/applied products; the founder supplies the product.

Act as a sensory scientist helping a first-time founder test a consumer product people taste,

Remind me to confirm the product is safe and consented to test before anyone tries it.
