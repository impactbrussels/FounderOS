# Sensory Methods (reference)

Scaled-down sensory-science protocols for a founder testing a tasted / smelled / felt / applied
product. Theme-agnostic across food, beverage, supplements, cosmetics, personal care, household.
**Verify methods against the primary standards** (ISO, ASTM, the texts at the end) and confirm
**safety, consent, and allergen disclosure** with a qualified expert before anyone tries a sample.

## The three families — pick by the decision

### 1. Discrimination — "is there a perceptible difference?"
Use a small **screened** panel (people with normal acuity, briefly oriented). Best when you
change an ingredient, supplier, or process and need to know if consumers would notice.

| Method | What assessors do | Use when |
|--------|-------------------|----------|
| **Triangle test** (ISO 4120) | 3 coded samples, 2 identical; pick the odd one | General "are A and B different?" |
| **Duo-trio** | A reference, then 2 coded; pick the one matching the reference | When you have a clear "standard" |
| **Paired comparison** | 2 coded samples; pick which is *more* of an attribute | Directional difference on one attribute |
| **Tetrad** | 4 samples grouped by similarity | More statistical power than triangle |

*Reading it:* compare the number of correct answers to the significance threshold for your `n`
(use the published critical-value tables — don't eyeball). More tasters → more power.

### 2. Descriptive — "how do they differ, in what attributes?"
Use a **trained or carefully oriented panel** to profile intensity of named attributes
(sweetness, bitterness, creaminess, fragrance, greasiness, etc.).
- **QDA / quantitative descriptive analysis** — rate each attribute on a line/intensity scale.
- **CATA (check-all-that-apply)** — a lighter, founder-friendly version: assessors tick which
  attributes apply; gives a usable profile without a fully trained panel.
*Output:* a profile/spider chart comparing your product vs a benchmark, attribute by attribute.

### 3. Affective / acceptance — "do target consumers like it / prefer it?"
Use **untrained target consumers** (your [ICP]) — never a trained panel (they over-analyse).

| Method | Scale / task | Notes |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| **9-point hedonic scale** | 1 = dislike extremely … 9 = like extremely | The workhorse acceptance scale (Peryam & Pilgrim) |
| **Paired preference** | "Which do you prefer, X or Y?" (forced choice) | Simple, decisive between two options |
| **JAR (Just-About-Right)** | "too little / just right / too much" per attribute | Tells you *which direction* to reformulate |

*Reading it:* a liking score is meaningless alone — interpret it against a **benchmark** (your
current product or the market leader) tested blind in the same session.

## Killing bias — the non-negotiables

1. **Blind & coded** — random 3-digit codes (e.g. 482, 715). Never "Sample A", never brand names.
2. **Randomised, balanced order** — counterbalance serving sequences across tasters to cancel
   first-position and carry-over effects.
3. **Controlled conditions** — identical temperature, portion size, vessel, lighting; isolate
   tasters (booths or separation) so they don't influence each other.
4. **Palate/sense reset** — water + plain cracker between food samples; adequate wait between
   fragrance/skin samples to avoid adaptation.
5. **Run blind to the hypothesis** — the administrator shouldn't signal the hoped-for answer.
6. **One job per panel** — discrimination/descriptive (screened) vs acceptance (target consumers).

## Sample size & decision rule (set before tasting)

- **Discrimination:** use the critical-value table for your method and `n`; pre-commit to the
  number of correct responses that means "different." Typical screening panels run a few dozen.
- **Acceptance:** ~50–100 target consumers for a reasonably stable liking read; under ~30 treat
  as **directional only**. Bigger when the decision is expensive or the difference is small.
- Write the rule first: *"If [result] ≥ [threshold] vs benchmark → proceed; else reformulate."*

## Test environments

- **CLT (Central Location Test)** — consumers test in a controlled venue; best control.
- **HUT (Home-Use Test)** — consumers use the product at home over days; best realism, less
  control. Use HUT when in-use experience over time matters (skincare, a daily food).

## Anti-patterns

- Tasting un-blinded, or with the founder watching hopefully.
- Asking "do you like it?" with no benchmark → a meaningless number.
- Letting tasters talk before scoring → herd effect.
- Tiny panels stated as proof.
- Mixing trained-panel description with consumer liking in one ask.
- Ignoring allergens, consent, or safety for ingested/applied products.

## Further reading (verify against primary sources)

ISO 4120 (triangle test), ISO 8589 (sensory test room design), ISO 11136 (consumer acceptance),
ISO 13299 (descriptive analysis); ASTM sensory standards; Peryam & Pilgrim (9-point hedonic
scale); Lawless & Heymann, *Sensory Evaluation of Food: Principles and Practices*; Stone & Sidel,
*Sensory Evaluation Practices* (QDA). Treat these as the authority; this reference is an
orientation, not a substitute for the standards or a qualified sensory professional.
