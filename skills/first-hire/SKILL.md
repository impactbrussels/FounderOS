# First Hire

> **Employment law, classification rules, and payroll/tax obligations vary by jurisdiction and
> change over time.** This skill helps you decide *who* to hire and *how* to hire them well - it
> is not legal, tax, or HR advice. Whether someone is legally an employee or a contractor, and
> what paperwork and obligations follow, depends on local law. **Confirm the specifics with a
> qualified local employment lawyer or accountant before you hire** (see
> `legal-and-incorporation`).

Your first hires set the company's bar, culture, and burn rate at once - and early hiring
mistakes are slow and expensive to fix, because a wrong hire in a five-person company is 20% of
it. First-timers tend to hire too early, hire for the wrong role, hire a version of themselves,
and "interview" on gut feel - then wonder why it didn't work. This skill makes the first hire
deliberate: hire only when it's the right move, for the one role that unblocks the biggest
constraint, against a written scorecard, through a structured process - and onboard so they
actually succeed.

## The method

Built on the FounderOS scaffold (High tier). Full decision tree, scorecard, question bank, and
checklists in [references/first-hires.md](references/first-hires.md).

### Step 1 - Decide if you should hire at all (ask before assuming)

A hire is the most expensive, least reversible way to add capacity - so make it the last
resort, not the reflex. Walk the decision tree (in the reference): can this be **automated**, a
**tool/process**, **outsourced** to a contractor or agency, **dropped** entirely, or is it
genuinely **not yet time**? Hire only when there's durable, full-time, core work that none of
those solve. Hiring to feel busy or less overwhelmed is the classic first-timer trap.

### Step 2 - Define the ONE role that unblocks the biggest constraint (batch then decide)

Don't hire for a wish-list of pains. Find your single biggest **constraint** - the bottleneck
that, if cleared, unlocks the most progress toward your `[GOAL]` - and define the **one role**
that clears it. Pick the highest-leverage single hire, not three half-roles. Be honest about
whether the constraint is really work you should still be doing yourself.

### Step 3 - Write a sharp role spec + scorecard (a draft is not a decision)

A job title is not a spec. Write a **scorecard**: the 3-5 concrete **outcomes** this person
must deliver in their first 6-12 months (measurable, e.g. against a `[METRIC]`), plus the
**competencies** and behaviours needed to deliver them. The scorecard - not a gut feeling - is
what you'll hire against, and it forces you to know what "great" looks like *before* you meet
anyone.

### Step 4 - Source candidates deliberately (research before asserting)

The best early hires usually come from your network and warm referrals, not a cold job board.
Source from several channels, write the role honestly (sell the mission *and* the reality of
early-stage), and cast wider than your own circle to avoid hiring a mini-me. Aim for a real
pipeline, not the first warm body.

### Step 5 - Run a structured interview + work-sample

Unstructured "let's chat" interviews mostly measure how much you like someone. Instead: ask the
**same structured questions** of every candidate (bank in the reference), score against the
scorecard, and - most predictive of all - run a **paid work-sample or trial project** that
mirrors the real job. What they actually produce beats how well they talk. Decide against the
scorecard, not the vibe.

### Step 6 - Decide employee vs. contractor at the concept level (verify locally)

Understand the *concept-level* trade-offs (in the reference) - control, commitment,
flexibility, cost, and risk - but **classification is a legal question**. Misclassifying a
worker carries real penalties, and the rules vary by jurisdiction. Form a leaning, then
**confirm the correct classification and paperwork with a local employment lawyer/accountant.**

### Step 7 - Onboard so they actually succeed

A great hire fails with a bad onboarding. Run the onboarding checklist (reference): context,
access/tools, the first-week and first-30/60/90-day outcomes, an owner to check in, and an
early honest feedback loop. The goal is a contributing, oriented teammate fast - not someone
left to "figure it out."

## Output

- **Hire / don't-hire decision**, with what you'll automate, outsource, drop, or defer instead - and why a full-time hire (if so).
- **The one role** that clears your biggest constraint, tied to your `[GOAL]`.
- **A role scorecard**: 3-5 measurable outcomes + the competencies to deliver them.
- **Sourcing plan + structured interview + work-sample** design, scored against the scorecard.
- **Employee-vs-contractor leaning** - explicitly flagged "confirm classification with a local legal/tax expert."
- **Onboarding plan**: first week and 30/60/90-day outcomes, owner, and feedback loop.

## Constraints

- **Hire last, not first.** Automate, outsource, simplify, or wait before adding a permanent headcount.
- **One role for the biggest constraint** - not a grab-bag of pains or three half-jobs.
- **Scorecard before candidates.** If you don't know what great looks like in writing, you'll hire on vibes.
- **Work-sample over interview charm.** What they produce predicts performance; how they chat doesn't.
- **Never DIY worker classification.** Employee vs. contractor is jurisdiction-specific law - verify with a local expert.
- Don't hire a mini-me. Stay theme-agnostic; the founder supplies the domain, you supply the rigour.
