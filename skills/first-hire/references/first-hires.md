# First Hires (reference)

Frameworks behind the `first-hire` skill. Theme-agnostic; the founder supplies the domain.

> **Employment law, worker-classification rules, and payroll/tax obligations vary by jurisdiction
> and change over time.** This is a hiring-craft reference, not legal, tax, or HR advice. Whether
> a worker is legally an employee or a contractor — and the paperwork, withholding, and
> obligations that follow — depends on local law, and misclassification carries real penalties.
> **Confirm specifics with a qualified local employment lawyer or accountant before hiring.**

## "Should you hire?" decision tree

Walk top to bottom. Hire only when you reach the bottom.

1. **Can it be DROPPED?** Is this work actually essential right now? Kill it if not.
2. **Can it be AUTOMATED or solved by a TOOL/PROCESS?** Software is cheaper and more reversible than a person.
3. **Can it be OUTSOURCED?** A contractor, freelancer, or agency for variable, specialised, or short-term work — no permanent headcount.
4. **Is it genuinely NOT YET TIME?** Can you do it yourself a while longer, or wait until the need is durable? Premature hiring burns runway and is hard to reverse.
5. **Only if none of the above:** is there durable, full-time, core work that needs an owner on the team? **Then hire.**

A hire is the most expensive, least reversible capacity you can add. Make it the last resort.

## Role-scorecard template

A title is not a role. Define the role by **outcomes**, then the **competencies** to deliver them.

**Role:** ____  **Reports to:** ____  **Why this role exists (the constraint it clears):** ____

**Mission (one sentence):** What this person is here to make true.

**Outcomes (3–5, measurable, first 6–12 months):**
| # | Outcome | How we'll measure it (e.g. [METRIC]) | By when |
|---|---------|--------------------------------------|---------|
| 1 | | | |
| 2 | | | |
| 3 | | | |

**Competencies (what it takes to hit the outcomes):**
- Must-have skills/experience: ____
- Behaviours / ways of working (e.g. ownership, resourcefulness, comfort with ambiguity): ____
- Culture-add (what they bring that we *lack* — not "fits in"): ____

**Deal-breakers:** ____

You hire against this scorecard. If you can't fill it in, you don't yet know what "great" looks like.

## Structured-interview question bank

Ask the **same** core questions of every candidate and score each against the scorecard. Examples — adapt to the role:

**Past performance (most predictive)**
- Walk me through your biggest accomplishment in a role like this. What was *your* part? How was it measured?
- Tell me about a time you owned an outcome end-to-end with little direction. What did you do?
- Describe a time you failed or missed a target. What happened, and what did you change?

**Role-specific competence**
- How would you approach [a real problem from our scorecard] in your first 30 days?
- What would you need from me to hit [outcome #1]?

**Ways of working & ambiguity**
- Tell me about working with very limited resources or information.
- How do you decide what to work on when everything feels urgent?

**Motivation & fit**
- Why this role, this stage, this problem — specifically?
- What would make you leave a job? (early-stage reality check)

Score 1–5 against scorecard competencies. Compare scores, not gut feelings.

## Work-sample / trial ideas (the strongest signal)

What a candidate *produces* predicts performance far better than how they interview. Keep it real, scoped, and **paid**.

| Role type | Work-sample idea |
|-----------|------------------|
| Builder / technical | A small, real (or realistic) task from your backlog; review the work and the reasoning. |
| Sales / growth | Have them prep and run a mock (or real, supervised) outreach/pitch for your actual offering. |
| Marketing / content | A short brief → a real deliverable (a post, a campaign outline) against your audience. |
| Ops / generalist | A messy real problem to untangle and propose a process for. |
| Any | A paid 1–3 day trial project that mirrors week-one work, with a clear brief and a debrief. |

Pay for substantial work-samples — it's fair, and it raises the seriousness on both sides.

## Employee vs. contractor — CONCEPT table (classification is a legal question — verify locally)

Concept-level trade-offs to inform your *leaning*. **The legal classification is NOT yours to decide
by preference** — it's set by local law based on the real working relationship, and getting it wrong
carries penalties. Confirm with a local expert.

| Dimension | Employee (concept) | Contractor (concept) |
|-----------|--------------------|----------------------|
| Control & integration | You direct how/when work is done; part of the team | They control how they deliver; engaged for a result |
| Commitment / continuity | Ongoing, full-time, durable role | Project- or time-bound, often part-time |
| Flexibility for you | Lower — harder/costlier to change | Higher — easier to scale up/down |
| Cost & obligations | Salary + employer obligations (payroll, benefits, protections — vary by country) | Usually a fee for work; fewer ongoing obligations — **but rules vary** |
| Risk | Misclassifying a true employee as a contractor = penalties | Same risk in reverse; depends on the real relationship, not the label |
| Best for | Durable, core, full-time work needing an owner | Variable, specialised, or short-term work |

**Do not pick the label to save money or paperwork.** The facts of the relationship decide it under
local law. Verify with a qualified employment lawyer/accountant.

## Onboarding checklist

A great hire fails with bad onboarding. Get them contributing and oriented fast.

**Before day one**
- [ ] Scorecard shared so they know what success looks like.
- [ ] Accounts, tools, access, and equipment ready.
- [ ] A simple first-week plan and a named go-to person.

**First week**
- [ ] Context: the mission, the customer, the current state, how decisions get made.
- [ ] One small, real, completable win to build momentum and confidence.
- [ ] Intro to everyone and how the team works.

**First 30 / 60 / 90 days**
- [ ] Clear outcomes for each checkpoint, tied to the scorecard.
- [ ] Regular check-ins; honest, early, two-way feedback (don't wait 90 days to course-correct).
- [ ] A frank early conversation if it's not working — for both sides.

## Anti-patterns

- **Hiring to feel busy / less overwhelmed** — adding headcount as emotional relief instead of solving the real constraint.
- **Hiring a mini-me** — picking someone with your exact skills, leaving the same gaps unfilled.
- **No scorecard** — hiring on vibes with no written definition of success, so you can't tell who's actually best.
- **Interview charm over work-sample** — mistaking likeability or smooth talk for ability.
- **Hiring too early** — a permanent salary before the need is durable, burning runway.
- **Three half-roles in one** — a job no one can actually do well, set up to fail.
- **DIY classification** — choosing employee/contractor by preference instead of verifying the law.
- **Hiring and abandoning** — no onboarding, then blaming the hire when they flounder.
