# Interview Guide (reference)

The frameworks behind the `customer-interviews` skill. Built on Mom Test discipline.
Theme-agnostic; the founder supplies the domain.

## The 3 Mom Test rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** Their life is fact you can learn from; your idea is
   bait for compliments. Never describe `[ONE_LINER]` until the learning is done.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future.** "Do you think
   you'd…?" is worthless. "When did you last…? What did you do?" is data.
3. **Talk less, listen more.** You're there to learn, not to be right. If you talked more than
   they did, you ran a pitch, not an interview.

## Question bank — good vs. leading

Steal these. The pattern: strip yourself and your solution out of the question.

| Goal | ❌ Leading / useless | ✅ Mom Test version |
|------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Confirm the problem exists | "Do you have a problem with `[PROBLEM]`?" | "Walk me through the last time `[PROBLEM]` came up for you." |
| Gauge severity | "Is `[PROBLEM]` really annoying?" | "What did that cost you — in time, money, or stress?" |
| Find the workaround | "Wouldn't a tool that did X be useful?" | "How do you handle it today? Show me your current setup." |
| Test demand | "Would you pay for a solution?" | "What have you already tried or paid for to fix this?" |
| Test frequency | "Does this happen a lot?" | "How many times did this happen last month?" |
| Find the real buyer | "Who would buy this?" | "Last time you bought something like this, who signed off?" |
| Surface priority | "Is this important to you?" | "What did you work on instead of fixing this?" |
| Get a commitment | "Cool, right?" | "Can I email you when there's something to try? Can I talk to your team?" |

**Openers that work:** "I'm trying to learn how people handle X — mind if I ask about your last
experience?" · "Tell me about how your day/workflow goes around X." · "What's the most
frustrating part of X?"

**Dig-deeper follow-ups (use relentlessly):** "When was the last time?" · "What did you do next?"
· "Why?" · "What were you using before?" · "How much did that cost you?" · "What else have you
tried?" · "Who else has this problem?"

## Signal vs. anti-signal table

| Real signal (write it down) | Anti-signal (discount it) |
|-----------------------------|---------------------------|
| A specific recent story, with detail | A vague generality ("I usually…") |
| Emotion — frustration, embarrassment, urgency | Polite enthusiasm ("cool!", "nice idea") |
| Money or time already spent on the problem | "I would definitely pay for that" (future tense) |
| An existing hacked-together workaround | "I'd use it if it were free / had feature X" |
| A concrete commitment (intro, time, pre-pay, pilot) | "Send me the link when it's live" (soft no) |
| They bring up the problem before you do | They only agree after you explain your idea |
| They get specific about who else has this | "Everyone has this problem" |

Rule of thumb: **compliments are anti-signals.** They feel like progress and aren't. The only
signals that matter cost the customer something — time, money, reputation, or a referral.

## Securing commitment (the ask that proves interest)

End strong conversations by asking for one of the three currencies. Escalating order:
- **Time** — "Can we talk again next week?" / "Can I shadow you doing X?"
- **Reputation** — "Can you intro me to two others with this problem?"
- **Money** — "Would you pre-order / pilot / sign a letter of intent?"

A "no" here is more useful than a "yes I'd use it" — it tells you the pain isn't real yet.

## Synthesis template (after 5–10 interviews)

> **Who we talked to:** [N] people matching [ICP screen].
> **Top 3 pains (in their words):**
> 1. "[verbatim quote]" — heard from [X/N]
> 2. "[verbatim quote]" — heard from [X/N]
> 3. "[verbatim quote]" — heard from [X/N]
> **Current workarounds:** [what they do today instead]
> **Commitments secured:** [intros / pilots / pre-orders / LOIs]
> **Language to reuse:** [the words customers used — for landing pages, positioning]
> **Read on `[RISKIEST_ASSUMPTION]`:**
> - **GO** if a clear majority told specific, emotional stories and ≥1 made a real commitment.
> - **PIVOT** if the pain is real but you're aimed at the wrong `[ICP]`, problem, or moment.
> - **KILL** if pain was generic, future-tense, and no one would commit. Bank the time saved.

## Anti-patterns

- **The pitch in disguise** — "research call" that's 80% you talking about your idea.
- **Fishing for compliments** — asking questions whose only honest answer is praise.
- **Leading the witness** — "Wouldn't it be great if…?" plants the answer you want.
- **Hypothetical futures** — trusting "I would" over "I did." Wishes aren't behaviour.
- **Ignoring the soft no** — "looks cool, send me the link" recorded as a yes.
- **Interviewing your fan club** — friends, family, and supporters who can't be neutral.
- **Not asking for commitment** — leaving without testing whether the interest is real.
- **No notes / no quotes** — paraphrasing from memory loses the customer's exact language.
