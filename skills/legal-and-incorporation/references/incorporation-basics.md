# Legal Foundations & Incorporation (reference)

Frameworks behind the `legal-and-incorporation` skill. Jurisdiction-agnostic; the founder supplies the country.

> ## ⚠️ This is general legal education, NOT legal advice.
> Nothing here is advice for your situation. **Company, tax, securities, employment, and IP law
> vary enormously by country and by state/province, change over time, and turn on facts not
> visible here.** Every threshold, rate, entity name, schedule, or "standard" below is an
> illustrative **concept to discuss with counsel** — not a fact to rely on. **Before acting,
> confirm specifics with a qualified lawyer and accountant licensed in your jurisdiction.**
> When in doubt, the answer is "ask a local lawyer."

## Legal foundations checklist

Stand these up early. Tick what's done; route the rest to counsel.

- [ ] **Entity formed** — the right vehicle for your goals and country (see concepts below), filed correctly.
- [ ] **Founders' agreement** — roles, decision rights, equity, vesting, what happens if someone leaves, IP, dispute resolution.
- [ ] **IP assignment** — every founder, contractor, and early collaborator has assigned work product to the company **in writing**.
- [ ] **Vesting** — all founder equity (yours included) vests over time, typically with a cliff. Confirm mechanics/tax with counsel.
- [ ] **Equity documents** — signed share/option paperwork; nothing held on a verbal promise or a spreadsheet alone.
- [ ] **Cap table** — one accurate, current record of who owns what (and what's promised).
- [ ] **Basic contracts** — customer/supplier terms, NDAs where genuinely warranted, contractor & employment paperwork.
- [ ] **Compliance basics** — registrations, tax IDs, required filings, beneficial-ownership/UBO records, data-protection duties — all confirmed locally.
- [ ] **Records & governance** — board/decision records kept; books reconcilable; signatures gathered, not "to be done later".

## Entity-type concepts (compare generically — names and rules differ by country)

Don't memorise entity names; compare on what actually matters, then confirm the right vehicle with counsel.

| Dimension | What to ask | Why it matters |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Liability protection** | Does this structure separate the company's debts/risks from my personal assets? | A separate legal person can shield personal assets; informal structures may not. |
| **Tax treatment** | Is the entity taxed itself, or does profit/loss pass through to me? What does that mean at my income level? | Tax is jurisdiction-specific and changes the real cost of each option. **Always confirm with an accountant.** |
| **Investor-readiness** | Can this entity issue the shares/options investors expect? Is it the "fundable" form in my country? | Venture investors usually expect a specific structure; the wrong one can mean an expensive conversion later. |
| **Admin burden & cost** | Filings, accounting, reporting, annual cost? | A heavier vehicle may not be worth it for a lifestyle/bootstrapped business — but may be essential for a venture path. |
| **Ownership flexibility** | Can I add co-founders, employees with options, and investors cleanly? | Some structures make adding owners or option pools awkward. |

Rough archetypes (concepts, **not** recommendations — the actual form differs by country):
- **Sole owner / informal** — cheapest, simplest; often little liability separation; rarely fundable. May suit very early solo testing.
- **Limited-liability company-type** — liability separation with lighter admin; tax/investor treatment varies widely.
- **Corporation-type (share-issuing)** — usually the path for venture funding and option pools; more admin and cost.

**Which of these is right — and what it's actually called — is a lawyer/accountant question.**

## The irreversible-mistakes list (the ones you can't cheaply undo)

These are the legal mistakes that are expensive or impossible to unwind. Treat them as red lines.

1. **No vesting on founder equity** — a founder leaves early owning a big, permanent slice. Always vest.
2. **Unassigned IP** — code/brand/designs legally owned by a founder, ex-contractor, or nobody. Blows up diligence; can sink a deal.
3. **Handshake equity splits** — undocumented ownership that becomes a vicious dispute exactly when the company gets valuable.
4. **Wrong or premature entity** — a structure that can't take investment or triggers bad tax, forcing a costly conversion.
5. **Mixing personal and company money** — can pierce liability protection and wreck the books. Separate accounts from day one.
6. **Issuing equity without proper paperwork / valuations** — securities and tax rules are unforgiving; get it papered correctly.
7. **Ignoring compliance** — missed filings, tax IDs, UBO/beneficial-ownership, or data-protection duties that compound into penalties.
8. **DIY templates on high-stakes documents** — a downloaded contract that doesn't fit your jurisdiction or your deal.

If you're near any of these, stop and call a lawyer **before** acting.

## "Template-OK vs. lawyer-required" rule of thumb

- **Often fine to start from a reputable template** (then sanity-check): simple NDAs, basic website/customer terms for low-risk offerings.
- **Get a lawyer to draft or review**: anything touching **equity, IP ownership, employment, investment, or significant money/liability**. The cost of getting these wrong dwarfs the legal fee.

## Anti-patterns

- **"We'll sort the legals later"** — later is during a fundraise or acquisition, when it's most expensive and least flexible.
- **Incorporating to feel legitimate** — before there's a co-founder, money, or IP to protect, it can be premature cost.
- **Trusting a verbal promise** — equity, IP, and roles unwritten are equity, IP, and roles in dispute.
- **Copying another country's setup** — "a US startup does X" tells you nothing about your jurisdiction.
- **Stating thresholds as fact** — quoting a tax rate or filing rule from memory. Always flag and verify locally.
