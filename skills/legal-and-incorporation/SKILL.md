# Legal and Incorporation

> **This is general legal education, NOT legal advice.** Nothing here is a recommendation for
> your situation. Company law, tax, securities, employment, and IP rules **vary enormously by
> country and by state/province**, change over time, and depend on facts this skill can't see.
> Do **not** rely on any threshold, rate, entity name, or "standard" mentioned here as fact -
> they are illustrative concepts to discuss. **Before you act, confirm the specifics with a
> qualified lawyer (and accountant) licensed in your jurisdiction.** A few hundred spent on a
> startup lawyer early is the cheapest insurance against mistakes that are expensive - or
> impossible - to unwind later.

Most legal mistakes founders make are invisible for years, then fatal exactly when it matters
most - at the first hire, the first investor, the first acquisition, or the first co-founder
falling out. The asymmetry is brutal: the right setup costs a little now; the wrong setup can
kill a fundraise or a sale later. This skill gets the foundations right early, when they're
cheap, and tells you precisely where you must stop guessing and call a lawyer.

### Step 1 - Decide whether and when to incorporate (research before asserting)

Incorporating creates a separate legal person that can own IP, sign contracts, take
investment, and shield your personal assets - but it also brings cost, filings, and reporting.
Common triggers to incorporate: you're taking outside money, hiring, signing meaningful
contracts, holding valuable IP, or working with a co-founder. **When** and **whether** depends
on your jurisdiction's rules - research the trigger points and costs locally, and confirm with
a lawyer before filing. Don't incorporate prematurely "to feel real"; don't delay it past the
first co-founder or first dollar of investment.

### Step 2 - Choose an entity type at the concept level (a draft is not a decision)

Compare entity options on the dimensions that actually matter (concepts in the reference):
**liability protection**, **tax treatment**, **investor-readiness**, and **admin burden**.
Different structures suit a lifestyle business, a bootstrapped company, and a venture-backed
startup. Form a *shortlist and a leaning* - then have a qualified local lawyer/accountant
confirm the right vehicle for your goals and country. The entity name that's "standard" for
fundable startups differs by jurisdiction; never assume.

### Step 3 - Assign founder IP to the company

This is the one founders regret most. Whatever you (and any contractor, designer, or early
collaborator) created for the startup - code, designs, brand, content - must be **legally
assigned to the company** in writing. Verbal "it's the company's" is not assignment. Missing
IP assignments routinely blow up due diligence and can sink a fundraise or acquisition. Get
proper assignment agreements drafted/reviewed by a lawyer.

### Step 4 - Set up founder vesting (ALWAYS)

Vesting means founders earn their equity over time (commonly with a cliff), so that a founder
who leaves in month three doesn't walk away owning a large slice forever. It protects the
people who stay and is expected by investors. **Always vest founder equity**, including your
own. The exact schedule and mechanics vary by jurisdiction and tax treatment - set them with a
lawyer, but never skip vesting.

### Step 5 - Keep a clean cap table and records (batch then decide)

Maintain a single, accurate **cap table** (who owns what), signed **equity documents**, board
and decision records, and the founders' agreement. Sloppy or contradictory records are a
classic deal-killer in diligence. Batch the foundational documents and have them reviewed
together rather than papering over gaps later.

### Step 6 - Know which contracts matter early (ask before assuming)

A short list of agreements does most of the protective work early: founders' agreement, IP
assignments, customer/supplier terms, an NDA where genuinely warranted, and proper
contractor/employee paperwork. Identify which you actually need now versus later - and where a
template is fine versus where you must have a lawyer draft or review. When unsure, ask the
lawyer; don't assume a downloaded template fits your jurisdiction.

## Output

- **Incorporate decision**: do it now or wait, with the trigger that decides it - flagged "confirm timing/cost with local lawyer."
- **Entity shortlist + leaning**, compared on liability / tax / investor-readiness / admin - explicitly marked as a draft to confirm with a lawyer.
- **IP assignment plan**: who created what, what must be assigned, and that a lawyer will paper it.
- **Vesting plan**: confirmed you'll vest all founders (schedule TBD with counsel).
- **Records to stand up now**: cap table, founders' agreement, equity docs, the early-contract shortlist.
- **The "call a lawyer now" list**: the specific items you will not DIY.

## Constraints

- **Never state a legal threshold, tax rate, filing rule, or "standard" entity as settled fact.** Flag it and route to a local lawyer.
- **Stay jurisdiction-agnostic.** Concepts and questions, not country-specific rules. The founder supplies the country; the lawyer supplies the law.
- **Always vest. Always assign IP in writing.** These two get founders into the most expensive trouble.
- Prefer a lawyer-reviewed document over a downloaded template wherever money, equity, IP, or employment is involved.
- A clean cap table and signed docs are not bureaucracy - they're what survives due diligence. Build them early.
