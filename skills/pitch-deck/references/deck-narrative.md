# Deck Narrative (reference)

The frameworks behind the `pitch-deck` skill. Theme-agnostic; the founder supplies the domain
and every number. A pitch deck is an argument delivered as slides — the order below is the
canonical order because it's the order an investor's questions arrive in.

> Note: fundraising norms, instruments and what a given stage "expects" vary by country, sector
> and investor type. Treat the conventions below as a strong default, not a rule. Confirm
> stage-specific expectations and any legal/financial terms with a lawyer or advisor.

## The canonical slide list — the single job of each slide

| # | Slide | Its ONE job | The headline should assert |
|---|-------|-------------|----------------------------|
| 1 | Title / one-liner | Make them want to read slide 2 | "[STARTUP_NAME]: [ONE_LINER]" — crisp, no jargon |
| 2 | Problem | Make the pain feel real and expensive | A specific [ICP] loses X because of [PROBLEM] |
| 3 | Why now | Justify urgency — why this didn't work 5 years ago and won't wait 5 more | A shift (tech, regulation, behaviour, cost curve) just opened the window |
| 4 | Solution | Show the insight, not the feature list | Our wedge solves [PROBLEM] in a way incumbents can't/won't |
| 5 | Product / how it works | Prove it's real and simple | One flow, one "aha" — a screen or diagram, not 12 features |
| 6 | Traction | Show evidence the dog eats the food | Real signal that's growing (see traction options below) |
| 7 | Market | Size the prize credibly | A reachable wedge inside a large, growing category — **sourced** |
| 8 | Business model | Show how money is made and that the unit math can work | We charge [who] [how] and the per-unit economics point the right way |
| 9 | Competition / why us | Position honestly and show your edge | Here's the landscape and the durable reason we win a slice |
| 10 | Team | De-risk execution | Why *this* team is unfairly suited to *this* problem |
| 11 | Ask | Make the next step unambiguous | Raising [amount] to hit [milestone] — see business-plan / financials |
| 12 | Vision / close | Leave them with the upside | If this works, the world looks like X |

Appendix (not in the 10–12): detailed financial model, cohort/retention curves, full
competitor matrix, GTM detail, cap table, pipeline. Pull these out only when a partner digs in.

## The story-arc template (write this before any slide)

Fill in eight sentences. Each must set up the next; if a beat doesn't connect, fix the argument
before touching design.

> 1. **Problem** — [ICP] is trying to [job] but [PROBLEM], and today they cope by [workaround],
>    which costs them [pain].
> 2. **Why now** — [shift] just happened, so this is suddenly possible / urgent.
> 3. **Solution** — [STARTUP_NAME] does [ONE_LINER], built on the insight that [insight].
> 4. **Traction** — and it's working: [METRIC] / [signal], growing [rate].
> 5. **Market** — the reachable wedge is [bottom-up estimate] inside a [sourced category].
> 6. **Business model** — we make money by [model], and the unit economics [direction].
> 7. **Team** — we're the team to do this because [unfair advantage].
> 8. **Ask** — so we're raising [amount] to reach [milestone] by [date].

If you can tell that as one spoken paragraph and it lands, the deck will land.

## What investors actually look for, per slide (skeptical lens)

| Slide | The objection they're hunting for | Pre-empt it by |
|-------|-----------------------------------|----------------|
| Problem | "Is this a real, urgent, frequent pain — or a vitamin?" | Evidence it hurts often and costs money |
| Why now | "Why hasn't this been done / why won't it wait?" | A concrete, recent inflection |
| Solution | "Is this a feature, not a company?" | The wedge + path to expansion |
| Product | "Does it actually exist / work?" | A real screen, demo link, or pilot |
| Traction | "Is this growth or noise? Vanity or value?" | Cohort/retention, paying or repeat behaviour |
| Market | "Is this TAM invented?" | Top-down AND bottom-up, both sourced |
| Business model | "Will the unit economics ever work?" | Honest per-unit math and assumptions |
| Competition | "What stops an incumbent crushing them?" | A durable, named edge — not "we have no competitors" |
| Team | "Can these people execute this specific thing?" | Domain fit, prior shipping, why-you |
| Ask | "What does this money buy?" | Amount tied to a specific milestone |

Different audiences weight differently: angels/pre-seed lean on **team + problem + why-now**
(little traction exists yet); Series A leans hard on **traction + unit economics + market**.
Calibrate emphasis to `[STAGE]` and audience.

## Traction without revenue (pre-revenue options)

No revenue is fine at early stages — show *some* evidence the dog eats the food, in roughly
descending strength:

- Signed **LOIs / pilots / design partners** (especially in B2B).
- **Pre-sales / deposits** — strangers paying before the product is finished.
- **Engaged usage + retention** — people coming back, not just signing up.
- **Waitlist with a real conversion signal** (not a raw vanity count).
- **Cohort behaviour** — week-over-week retention beats total-signups.
- **Letters, quotes, or champions** from real target [ICP] customers.
- **Channel proof** — a repeatable, affordable way you acquire users.

Rank by behaviour and money. "Likes," press, and "lots of interest" are not traction.

## Common deck mistakes (and the fix)

- **Feature catalogue, no narrative** → build the arc first; slides serve the arc.
- **Five messages per slide** → one headline message each; the rest supports it.
- **Invented TAM** → source top-down + bottom-up, cite it, or label it a placeholder. A made-up
  market number is an instant credibility hit.
- **"We have no competitors"** → reads as "I haven't looked." The alternative is always
  status-quo, spreadsheets, or doing nothing — name it.
- **Burying the ask** → state amount + milestone plainly; don't make them hunt.
- **Weak "why now"** → without urgency, the deck is "interesting," which is a soft no.
- **Vanity metrics** → swap total signups for retention/paying behaviour.
- **No appendix** → questions you can't answer fast look like gaps; pre-load the appendix.
- **Designing before deciding** → a draft is not a decision; batch edits, then cut once.

## On numbers (read this twice)

Every figure in the deck is a claim you'll be asked to defend. Market size especially: build it
**bottom-up** (units × price × reachable customers) *and* **top-down** (credible category size),
and **cite the source** for the top-down number. If you don't have a source yet, write
`[SOURCE — verify]` on the slide and get it before you send the deck. Never invent a number to
fill a slide; a wrong number a partner catches poisons the whole deck.
