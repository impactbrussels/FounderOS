# Positioning & GTM Playbook (reference)

Frameworks behind the `positioning-and-gtm` skill. Theme-agnostic; the founder supplies the domain.

## Positioning statement template (April Dunford structure)

Build positioning from five components, in this order — each one is an input to the next:

| # | Component | Question it answers | Example slot |
|---|-----------|---------------------|--------------|
| 1 | **Competitive alternatives** | What would `[ICP]` use if you didn't exist? (incl. spreadsheet / manual / nothing) | "...today they cope with a spreadsheet and three Slack threads" |
| 2 | **Unique attributes** | What do you have that the alternatives don't? | "...auto-reconciles in real time" |
| 3 | **Value (so what)** | What does that attribute let them *do* or *avoid*? | "...so they close the books in a day, not a week" |
| 4 | **Who cares most** | Which `[ICP]` segment values that the most? | "...finance leads at 10–50-person startups" |
| 5 | **Market category** | What frame sets the right expectations and comparison set? | "...a close-automation tool, not 'accounting software'" |

**The one-liner that falls out:**

> For **[ICP]** who **[PROBLEM]**, **[STARTUP_NAME]** is a **[market category]**
> that, unlike **[competitive alternatives]**, delivers **[unique value]**.

Draft three versions with **different market categories** — the category changes who you're
compared to and which strengths look impressive. Pick the frame that makes your moat obvious.

## Attributes → value translation (the "so what" ladder)

For every feature, ask "so what?" until you hit an outcome the `[ICP]` would pay for.

| Attribute (true about us) | So what? (capability) | So what? (outcome `[ICP]` cares about) |
|---------------------------|-----------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Real-time sync | No manual exports | Decisions on today's numbers, not last week's |
| One-click setup | No onboarding project | Live in an afternoon, not a quarter |
| SOC 2 compliant | Passes security review | Enterprise can actually buy you |

Keep only rows where rivals **can't** easily say the same thing. The rest are table stakes.

## Channel menu with fit signals

Pick **1–2**. The fit signal is the evidence that the channel is worth a real test.

| Channel | Fit signal (pick it if…) | Typical motion |
|---------|--------------------------|----------------|
| Cold outbound (email/DM) | High-value, identifiable `[ICP]`; you know who they are by name | Sales-led |
| SEO / content | `[ICP]` searches for the problem; long sales cycle; you can write with authority | Content-led |
| Communities (Slack/Discord/Reddit/forums) | `[ICP]` already gathers there and trusts peers over ads | Community-led |
| Paid ads (search/social) | Cheap-ish CAC, clear intent or tight targeting, you can measure to `[METRIC]` | Self-serve |
| Marketplaces / directories (app stores, integration listings) | Buyers shop there with intent; platform sends qualified traffic | Self-serve |
| Founder-led social (LinkedIn/X) | Your `[ICP]` follows operators; you'll post consistently for months | Content/community |
| Partnerships / resellers | Someone already sells to your `[ICP]` and you're complementary | Sales-led |
| Events / field | High-ticket deals; trust needs a face; `[ICP]` attends | Sales-led |

## GTM-motion selector

| Motion | Best when | Tell-tale `[METRIC]` |
|--------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Self-serve** | Low price (<~$100/mo), simple value, user can buy alone | Signup → activation → paid conversion |
| **Sales-led** | High ACV (>~$10k), multiple stakeholders, custom needs | Meetings → opportunities → close rate |
| **Community-led** | Identity/peer-driven `[ICP]`, network effects, low budget | Members → engaged → activated |
| **Content-led** | Searchable problem, long consideration, you can teach | Traffic → email → trial → paid |

Most early startups run **one** motion well. Mixing self-serve and sales-led at seed stage usually
means doing both badly. Match the motion to price and `[CONSTRAINT]`, not to ambition.

## Messaging hierarchy (the source of truth)

```
HEADLINE        → positioning statement in plain human words (the promise)
VALUE PILLAR 1  → proof: metric / demo / testimonial / logo
VALUE PILLAR 2  → proof: ...
VALUE PILLAR 3  → proof: ...
```

Every landing page, pitch, ad, and cold email inherits from this. If a claim isn't in the
hierarchy, it doesn't go in the asset. Three pillars max — more dilutes the headline.

## Anti-patterns

- **Boil-the-ocean channels** — running 6 channels at 10% effort instead of 2 at full effort. You learn nothing and burn budget.
- **Feature-led messaging** — "we have X, Y, Z." Customers buy outcomes; features belong in the proof layer.
- **Positioning against a fantasy competitor** — ignoring that the real rival is a spreadsheet or inertia.
- **Wrong market category** — letting your tech name your category and dragging yourself into a brutal comparison set.
- **Channel by convenience** — posting where *you* are comfortable, not where `[ICP]` already is.
- **No kill criterion** — running a channel forever because stopping feels like failure. Set a `[METRIC]` bar up front.
- **Positioning by committee** — averaging everyone's opinion into mush. Pick a sharp stance and test it.
