# Positioning and Go-To-Market

A first-time founder's instinct is to describe the product by its features and then try every
channel at once. Both fail. Weak positioning makes customers do the work of figuring out why
they should care - most won't. Spreading across ten channels means doing all of them badly.
This skill forces the two decisions that matter most before launch: **what you are in the
customer's mind**, and **the one or two places you'll go to find them**.

## The method

Built on the Founder OS scaffold (High tier). Full frameworks in
[references/positioning.md](references/positioning.md).

### Step 1 - Pin the ICP and the alternative they use today

Positioning is relative. You are not positioning in a vacuum - you're positioning against what
`[ICP]` does *right now* to solve `[PROBLEM]`. Name the **competitive alternatives** honestly:
a rival product, a manual workaround, a spreadsheet, a hire, or doing nothing. If you don't know
the real alternative, you can't claim to be better - research it first (the founder supplies the
domain; you supply the rigour).

### Step 2 - Find the unique value, not the feature list

List your attributes (what's true about the product), then translate each into **value** (what
it lets the customer do or avoid). Keep only the attributes that competitive alternatives *can't*
easily claim. The intersection of "true about us" and "rivals can't say this" and "`[ICP]` cares"
is your differentiated value. Everything else is table stakes - mention it, don't lead with it.

### Step 3 - Write the one-line positioning statement

Use the April Dunford structure (template in references): for `[ICP]` who `[PROBLEM]`,
`[STARTUP_NAME]` is a `[market category]` that, unlike `[alternatives]`, delivers `[unique value]`.
The **market category** is a strategic choice - it sets the customer's expectations and the
comparison set. Pick the frame that makes your strengths obvious. Draft three; pick one.
A draft is not a decision - test the line on a real `[ICP]` before you commit.

### Step 4 - Build the messaging hierarchy

One headline (the positioning statement in human words), three supporting value pillars, and the
proof under each (metric, demo, testimonial, or logo). This is the source of truth every landing
page, pitch, and ad inherits from. Lead with value and outcome, never with features.

### Step 5 - Pick 1-2 channels and one GTM motion

Match `[CHANNEL]` to where `[ICP]` *already is*, not where it's easy to post. Use the channel
menu + fit signals in references; pick at most two and commit. Then pick the **motion** -
self-serve, sales-led, community-led, or content-led - that fits your price point, deal size,
and `[CONSTRAINT]`. Batch the channel experiments, then decide based on `[METRIC]`, not vibes.

## Output

- **Positioning statement** (one line, Dunford structure) + the two runner-up framings and why you rejected them.
- **Messaging hierarchy**: headline, 3 value pillars, proof under each.
- **Channel choice**: 1-2 channels with the fit signal that justifies each, and the ones you're deliberately *not* doing.
- **GTM motion** with a one-line rationale tied to price/deal-size/`[CONSTRAINT]`.
- **First experiment**: what you'll test on which channel, the `[METRIC]`, and the bar to continue vs. cut.

## Constraints

- Position **against the real alternative**, including "do nothing" - not against an imaginary competitor.
- Lead with **value and outcome**, never a feature dump. Features go in the proof layer.
- Pick **at most two channels**. "Be everywhere" is a budget for failing everywhere.
- The market category is a deliberate choice, not the literal name of your tech.
- Stay theme-agnostic; the founder supplies the domain, you supply the structure.

## Copy-paste version

```
Act as a sharp B2B/B2C positioning and go-to-market strategist for a first-time founder.
My product: [ONE_LINER]. Who it's for: [ICP]. The problem it solves: [PROBLEM].
My constraints (budget/time/team): [CONSTRAINT].
Help me:
1. Name the REAL alternatives my [ICP] uses today (including spreadsheets, workarounds, and doing nothing).
2. Turn my features into VALUE, and keep only the value that rivals can't easily claim and my [ICP] actually cares about.
3. Write 3 one-line positioning statements in this shape: "For [ICP] who [PROBLEM], [STARTUP_NAME] is a [market category] that, unlike [alternatives], delivers [unique value]." Then recommend one and tell me why.
4. Give me a messaging hierarchy: 1 headline, 3 value pillars, and the proof I'd need under each.
5. Recommend the 1-2 channels where my [ICP] already hangs out, and ONE go-to-market motion (self-serve / sales-led / community / content) that fits my price and constraints. Tell me what NOT to do.
Don't let me lead with features, and don't let me pick more than two channels.
```
