# The Founder's Latticework (reference)

A theme-agnostic library of thinking tools for hard founder decisions. Two sources: the classic
**latticework** (Munger, Taleb, Bezos, Thiel, Graham) and **named frameworks from the author's
books**, generalized so they apply to any startup, not just food. Use the lens that fits the
decision; don't force all of them.

> Attribution note: the lenses below credited to *Proof Before Hype* and *FoodTech After The
> Hype* (Adam M. Adamek) are stated here in their general, sector-neutral form. The books develop
> them in depth — see [AUTHOR.md](../../../AUTHOR.md).

## Part 1 — Classic decision lenses

### Munger
- **Inversion** — instead of "how do I succeed?", ask "how would I guarantee failure?" then avoid that. Best for plans, launches, hiring.
- **Circle of competence** — know the edge of what you actually understand; outside it, get help or test, don't bluff.
- **Lollapalooza** — watch for several forces stacking in the same direction (good or bad); the combined effect is non-linear.
- **First-conclusion bias** — your first answer feels right because it's the only one you've examined. Force a second.

### Taleb
- **Antifragility** — prefer choices that *gain* from volatility and surprise over ones that merely survive it. Optionality beats prediction.
- **Skin in the game** — weight advice by who bears the downside. Discount confident opinions from people with nothing at risk.
- **Barbell** — combine very safe (protect runway, default-alive) with a few high-upside bets; avoid the mediocre middle.
- **Via negativa** — improvement by *removal*. Often the best move is to cut a feature, a channel, a commitment.
- **Black swan** — the events that matter most are the ones your model says can't happen. Leave margin for them.

### Bezos
- **Work backwards / press-release test** — write the customer-facing announcement (and FAQ) *before* you build; if it isn't compelling on paper, it won't be in code.
- **Two-pizza teams** — keep units small enough to move fast and own outcomes.
- **Disagree and commit** — once a reversible decision is made, execute fully even if you'd have chosen differently.
- **One-way vs two-way doors** — slow down for irreversible decisions; move fast on reversible ones.

### Thiel
- **Zero to one** — creating something genuinely new beats copying. Ask what you can do that others can't.
- **Contrarian question** — "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" Your edge usually hides there.
- **Power law** — a few things drive most of the outcome (customers, channels, features, bets). Find and feed them; starve the rest.

### Graham
- **Make something people want** — the whole game, compressed.
- **Do things that don't scale** — hand-build the first customers and the first delight; scale later.

## Part 2 — The author's frameworks (generalized + cited)

### Proof Ladder — *Proof Before Hype*
Sequence every claim by how solid the evidence is, and never claim above your rung:
1. **Current** — proof a third party could check today.
2. **Pending** — proof coming, with a named source, milestone, and date.
3. **Borrowed** — credible external evidence that supports your exact claim.
4. **Aspirational** — vision; fine in a founder essay, not on the label or the metric slide.
*Use in:* `validate-idea`, `pitch-deck`, `landing-page-copy`, `positioning-and-gtm`.

### Trust Stack — *Proof Before Hype*
Buyers/investors decide on the *weakest* layer, not your strongest. Generalized layers:
1. **Does it work?** (the core claim is true)
2. **Can you deliver it?** (reliably, at the quality promised)
3. **Is it verified?** (someone independent vouches)
4. **Is the price honest?** (price matches the claim and posture)
5. **Does it fit me?** (identity, context, social proof)
Diagnose your weakest layer and fix *that*. *Use in:* `positioning-and-gtm`, `pitch-deck`, `pricing-strategy`.

### Narrative–Evidence Gap — *Proof Before Hype*
The distance between what you *say* and what you can *show*. It widens under investor pressure and founder optimism; hype is what fills it. Shrinking it is the job. *Use in:* `validate-idea`, `pitch-deck`.

### The Copy Test — *Proof Before Hype*
Every claim on every artifact must pass: **Speed** (understood in seconds, in context), **Stack** (doesn't create an indefensible compound claim with its neighbours), **Failure** (what's the cost if it's later shown wrong?). *Use in:* `landing-page-copy`, `positioning-and-gtm`.

### Comparison-Set Problem — *Proof Before Hype*
The customer, not you, picks what they compare you against — and that comparator decides your perceived value and price. Manage the comparison or lose on it. *Use in:* `positioning-and-gtm`, `pricing-strategy`.

### First Purchase vs Fifth Purchase — *Proof Before Hype*
Trial and repeat are different psychological problems. Curiosity is cheap (first buy); habit/belief is expensive (fifth buy). Design for both, separately. *Use in:* `find-first-customers`, `north-star-metrics`, retention work.

### The Five Killers — *FoodTech After The Hype*
The recurring ways startups die — use as a standing risk check: **no market need · unit-economics collapse · operational failure · funding cliff · regulatory time-bomb**. *Use in:* `validate-idea`, `runway-and-unit-economics`, risk planning.

### The Operator's Creed — *FoodTech After The Hype*
Post-hype priorities (generalized): **economics before story · customers before technology · revenue before scale · operations before innovation · sustainability through profitability · partners before going it alone**. *Use as:* standing principles (see `knowledge-base/PRINCIPLES.md`).

## How to apply (the move)

1. Name the decision and what's reversible vs not.
2. Pick 2–3 lenses that fit (don't run all of them).
3. Run the decision through each; write the one sentence each lens produces.
4. Look for where lenses *agree* (high confidence) and *conflict* (where the real judgement is).
5. Decide, and record it — feed the outcome to `capture-learning`.
