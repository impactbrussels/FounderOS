For twenty-five years, my job was to be the person in the room who asked the uncomfortable
question. In food and biotech R&D, a confident guess that skips the evidence doesn't cost you an
afternoon - it costs a launch, a line, sometimes a company. You learn to want proof before you
want applause.

Then I did the thing I'd spent a career helping other people do. I started building. I taught
myself to work with AI - Python, model training, shipping in the open - and became the
first-time founder I used to advise from the safe side of the table.

It humbled me fast.

Not because the work was hard in the way I expected. Because I kept hitting the same wall, and
it wasn't a *how* wall. I knew how to run an experiment, how to read a P&L, how to write. The
wall was *which*. Which thing do I do **this week**? Validate or build? Talk to ten customers or
ship the landing page? Raise now or wait? And underneath all of it, the question that 25 years
had drilled into me: **how do I tell a real signal from hype - including my own?**

The advice exists. Of course it does. It's just scattered across a hundred books, ten thousand
threads, and a small mountain of $5,000 courses - and none of it is wired into the tools I
actually build in. A first-time founder doesn't need more content. They need to know what to do
next, and a way to keep themselves honest while they do it.

So I built that. It's called **Founder OS**, and as of this week it's open and free.

## What it actually is

Founder OS is an operating system for first-time founders - the founder journey encoded as
runnable AI skills. Idea → validation → MVP → launch → fundraising → operations. Twenty-two deep
skills today, each one a sharp, opinionated method for a single job: pressure-test an idea, run
honest customer interviews, scope the smallest real MVP, price it, write the deck, model the
runway, make the first hires.

You can run it inside AI coding tools (Claude Code, Codex, Cursor), or - and this matters - you
can use it with **zero setup**. Every skill ends with a copy-paste prompt you drop into ChatGPT,
Claude, or Gemini, fill in your details, and go. If you can paste, you can run the whole
playbook. There's a `start-here` skill that asks you three questions and tells you the one thing
to do next, because the worst place for a founder to be is staring at a menu of twenty-two
options.

And it carries one rule, the one I brought from the lab: **proof before hype.** Validate before
you build. Cite before you claim. A draft is never a decision. It's theme-agnostic on purpose -
software, services, or a new protein, you bring the domain and Founder OS brings the method.

## The part that's for us, the food people

Here's the thing I believe more strongly the longer I do this: in food and biotech, you can have
*great* science and still lose the market. Most founders I meet don't have a data problem. They
have a **belief** problem. Investors, regulators, and buyers can't see what the science unlocks
in the real world - so it doesn't matter that the protein works if nobody trusts it, tries it,
or repeats.

That's the spine of my writing, and it's baked into Founder OS as general frameworks any founder
can use:

- **The Proof Ladder** - never claim above your evidence. Sort every claim into what you can
  show today, what's coming with a date, what you've borrowed from a credible source, and what's
  still just vision. Put the vision in your essays, not on the pack.
- **The Trust Stack** - buyers decide on your *weakest* layer, not your strongest: does it work,
  can you deliver it, is it verified, is the price honest, does it fit me? Find your weak layer
  and fix *that*.
- **First purchase vs fifth purchase** - curiosity is cheap and habit is expensive; they're
  different problems, and most startups only design for the first.

None of these are food-specific. That's the point. The discipline travels; the domain is yours.

## Why it's open, and why I'm asking for two things

I don't think the operating knowledge of building a company should be gatekept and sold back to
the people who most need it and can least afford it. So Founder OS is open source,
attribution-only. Take it. Use it. Build something that matters.

Two asks, both honest:

**Contribute.** It ships with 22 skills and a roadmap of about 85 more - pricing experiments,
retention, hiring, board prep, grant-finding - each one a "good first issue." If you've built or
advised a startup, you know something the next founder doesn't. Come teach it. Your name stays
on it.

**Sponsor, if it's yours to give.** Sponsorship is what keeps Founder OS free and independent
instead of course-walled. If your company sells to founders, it puts you in front of them at the
moment they're deciding. One rule I won't bend: sponsorship funds the commons; it never buys a
recommendation inside a skill. That line is the whole point.

## Where to start

If you're building anything right now, do this: open the repo, run `start-here` (or paste its
prompt into your chatbot), and tell it what you're working on. It'll point you at the one next
move. Then go make that move.
