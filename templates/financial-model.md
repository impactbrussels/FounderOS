## B. Unit economics (per customer)

A "unit" is one customer. The question: does one customer make or lose money?

| Metric | Formula | Your value |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| **Avg revenue per customer / month (ARPU)** | total monthly revenue ÷ active customers | |
| **Gross margin %** | (revenue − cost to serve) ÷ revenue | |
| **Avg customer lifetime (months)** | 1 ÷ monthly churn rate | |
| **LTV** (lifetime value) | ARPU × gross margin % × lifetime months | |
| **CAC** (acquisition cost) | (sales + marketing spend) ÷ new customers won | |
| **LTV : CAC ratio** | LTV ÷ CAC | |
| **Payback period (months)** | CAC ÷ (ARPU × gross margin %) | |

**Read-outs (rules of thumb — they vary, don't treat as targets):**
- **LTV:CAC** — often considered healthy around ≥ 3:1. Below 1:1 you lose money on every customer.
- **Payback** — shorter is safer; many subscription businesses aim for under ~12 months.
- **Churn** is the silent killer — a small monthly churn compounds into a short lifetime.

---

## C. The three questions this model answers

1. **How long do I have?** → Runway (Section A). If it's short, that's your #1 constraint.
2. **Does each customer make money?** → Unit economics (Section B). If no, fix this before scaling.
3. **Can I afford to grow?** → CAC vs. cash. Growth you can't fund is a trap, not traction.

> A model is a set of assumptions, not a forecast. Write your assumptions down next to the numbers
> so you can see which one breaks the plan when reality disagrees.
